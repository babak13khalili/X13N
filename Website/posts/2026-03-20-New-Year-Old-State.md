---
title: New Year, Old State
written: 12-03-2026
---

**Friday – 11:26**

Moment by moment, we are getting closer to the Persian New Year.
Only a few hours remain.

For years, ever since I first imagined migrating, one of the most vivid situations I pictured myself in was this exact moment:
my first New Year away from home and family.

For years, I constructed different scenarios.
I saw myself in that moment, likely experiencing a strange blend of sadness, longing, and joy, sitting in front of my computer, neatly dressed, in a clean and carefully arranged room,
beside a Haft-Seen table I had set myself, connected to my family online.

Or perhaps, I had managed to travel back home for these days, to be there, physically present.

All those years, I imagined this moment in every possible way.
except this.

Alone.
Angry.
Sad.
Exhausted.

No Haft-Seen.
No sense of appearance or preparation.

And not even the certainty of being able to contact my family.

For the first time this year, I saw no Haft-Seen,
no Sabzeh, no Goldfish, no Hyacinths
and naturally, no New Year gifts.

Alone, in a safe, beautiful, and calm country, far from friends, family, and everything familiar.
In safety. In peace.

And yet, a thought keeps looping in my mind:

When will I next be able to return to my country?
And when I do, how much will still resemble what I left behind?

My favorite places.
Streets.
Cafés and restaurants I used to visit regularly.
People.

I find no answers.
And I hate not knowing. I hate uncertainty.

It feels as if my entire life is currently suspended in its most undefined, unstable state.

I have never felt this uncertain about anything.

There is almost nothing in my life right now that feels fixed or definite.
Everything I look at is ambiguous, unresolved.

Meanwhile, my country and my people are enduring some of the heaviest traumas and atrocities.
We had not yet processed the unprecedented repression and violence inflicted by the regime, when another war began.

Even describing the contradictory emotional states we are experiencing feels nearly impossible.

I have tried, many times, to separate events and analyze them, and each time, I arrive at helplessness.

I wonder:

How much can we endure?
Psychologically. Physically.

It feels as though life has turned into an experiment, to observe the limits of my own endurance.

The harsher things become,
the more I expect myself to continue
to survive creatively.

But I keep asking:

Until when?
To what extent does this elasticity hold?

What else can we experience and still continue?
What meanings can collapse in our minds, and still, we move forward, searching for new ones?

How many times will we ever experience this life, to be forced into enduring such weight?

Despite everything, each year, certain occasions, especially the New Year, have always been symbolic checkpoints for me:
a moment to reflect, to learn, to forget, and to begin again.

But this year, for the first time, that sense of “new beginning” is not automatic.
I am forcing it.

The things I feel resentment, anger, frustration, and helplessness toward
clearly outweigh the things that give me hope and motivation.

And for the first time, I am not afraid to say it.

I am not afraid to admit that the scale is heavier on darkness than on light.

I do not want to perform hopefulness just because today is supposed to be symbolic.
On the contrary.

I want to gift myself the courage to say, clearly and firmly, even to my small circle:
I am angry.
I am sad.
I am exhausted.

And I am not ashamed of it.

I am repulsed by slogans.
By symbolic phrases.
By hollow congratulations and ritualistic wishes.

Nothing is okay.

I feel trapped in a psychological battlefield between conflicting political, religious, and social ideologies and I am exhausted by all of them.

People who selectively choose which atrocities to acknowledge and which to ignore, based on their ideological preferences.

I am disgusted by all of it.

People who, because of their beliefs, become thirsty for the blood of those who disagree with them, who threaten, who justify, who ignore the wrongdoings of their own side while amplifying those of the other.

Death is death.
Crime is crime.

What difference does it make what justification stands behind it?

In every case, without hesitation,
any form of injustice is condemnable.

How can you not see this, when, in the end, it is always us, the people,
who pay the price?

Mentally.
Emotionally.
Financially.
With our lives.

I am tired of the repetitive cycle of
“may this year never return,”
and the illusion of hope projected onto a better year.

I am tired of the constant tension in my throat, sometimes spreading to my head, my entire body.
I am tired of suppressed anger.
I am tired of the overwhelming flood of news, propaganda, predictions, analyses, announcements.
I am tired of numbers, names, statistics.
I am tired of emotional instability.
I am tired of helplessness.
I am tired of constantly feeling like I should be doing something.
I am tired of being tired.

The Gregorian New Year came with grief and anger over state violence.
The Persian New Year arrives with grief, anger, and the anxiety of war.

And somehow, I came across this line of poetry:

“The delightful spring has arrived, yet the heart is not in place.”


**20 March 2026 – 12:45**
**Home, Groningen — the final hours of the Persian year**
